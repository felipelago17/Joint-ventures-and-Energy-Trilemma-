# 4A. Accounting, Risk, and Project-Governance Foundations of JV-Based Oil M&A

The preceding section established *why* firms route oil and gas M&A through joint-venture
(JV) interest transfers rather than outright asset sales. That strategic account, however,
rests on three substrata it does not itself examine: how JV interests are recognised in the
accounts, what operational risk and management-system obligations travel with them, and how
the wider capital-project governance literature explains the dominance of the JV form. This
section supplies that substrate and links each dimension to the energy trilemma of security,
affordability, and sustainability that frames this research.

## 4A.1 Accounting Classification and the Valuation of JV Interests

The financing constraints flagged earlier — reserve-based lenders discounting interests that
convey a share of production rather than reserve ownership, and the resulting valuation gaps
— have a concrete accounting mechanism in IFRS 11. The standard classifies a joint
arrangement by the parties' rights and obligations rather than its legal form: a *joint
operation* confers direct rights to assets and obligations for liabilities, recognised
line-by-line in proportion to the interest held, whereas a *joint venture* confers rights
only to the net assets of a separate vehicle and is reported through the equity method as a
single net-investment line.[^1] The distinction is decisive for an acquirer. A buyer of a
joint-operation interest brings its share of reserves, production assets, and
decommissioning liabilities directly onto its own balance sheet, with the corresponding
effect on gearing and on the asset base available as collateral. A buyer of an
equity-accounted JV interest consolidates none of this; it reports only a net investment,
leaving lenders with weaker visibility of the underlying reserves against which
reserve-based lending is advanced. This is the accounting source of the higher cost of
capital and the valuation gaps the strategic analysis attributes to concession structures.

The treatment of pre-production expenditure compounds the point. IFRS 6 permits entities to
capitalise exploration and evaluation outlay under an elected accounting policy, subject to
impairment testing once technical feasibility and commercial viability are assessed.[^2] In
farm-in transactions — the staged transfers central to the strategic analysis of the UK
Continental Shelf — the carrying value of capitalised exploration and evaluation assets, and
whether a farminee's carry of costs is itself capitalised, directly shape both the
consideration and the design of any earn-out. Accounting policy, in other words, is not a
back-office matter but a determinant of how a JV-based deal is priced.

Both jurisdictions operate within the IFRS framework: UK-listed independents and the
ADNOC-listed entities alike report under IFRS, so the *standards* converge even where the
*structures* diverge. UK unincorporated joint operating agreements tend toward
joint-operation accounting, while interests held through incorporated operating companies
— the more common UAE pattern, in which ADNOC retains a majority and the international
partner a minority — are more likely to fall to equity accounting for that minority. The
precise classification turns on the specific rights and obligations each contract confers
and cannot be assumed from form alone. Published financial statements from ADNOC-listed
entities indicate that midstream and downstream joint ventures — such as the AGP JV (Abu
Dhabi Gas Projects Joint Venture) and ALNG JV, held through ADNOC Gas PLC — are
equity-accounted as associates, consistent with the joint-venture classification where
rights attach to the net assets of a separate vehicle rather than directly to underlying
reserves and production assets.[^11] Whether this reasoning extends to upstream concession
interests held by IOC minority partners (such as the Ghasha concession interests of Eni,
PTTEP, and OMV) turns on whether those interests are held through an incorporated
operating company — pointing toward the equity method — or through an unincorporated
joint operating agreement conferring direct pro-rata rights to reserves and production —
pointing toward joint-operation line-by-line recognition.[^12] In the upstream sector, the
unincorporated JOA is the predominant structure and typically produces joint-operation
accounting, but the determination for specific ADNOC concession instruments remains
contingent on the concession documentation itself.

## 4A.2 Risk and HSE Management Systems as Integration and Due-Diligence Factors

Where the host paper observes that due diligence must now reach beyond legal compliance,
the operational counterpart of that claim lies in the management-system standards an
incoming partner or operator inherits. Three are material. ISO 31000 sets out the
principles, framework, and process for enterprise risk management; though not a certifiable
standard, it is the benchmark against which a target's risk maturity is assessed.[^3]
ISO 45001 establishes a certifiable occupational health and safety management system whose
obligations — and exposures — transfer with operatorship, a consideration that becomes
acute in late-life and decommissioning assets where safety liability is concentrated.[^4]
The upstream-specific architecture is provided by the IOGP Operating Management System
framework (Report 510), widely adopted across the sector to integrate leadership, risk, and
asset-integrity controls into a single operating system.[^5]

These standards reframe the operatorship clauses examined in the next section. Because the
operating management system and the HSE regime travel with operatorship, a buyer assuming
the operator role assumes not only contractual control but the asset-integrity and safety
obligations the management system encodes; a buyer remaining a non-operator must instead
satisfy itself that the incumbent operator's system is robust. Management-system maturity
therefore becomes a due-diligence object in its own right, distinct from title and from
the ESG screening discussed later.

The comparative contrast is sharp. In the UAE, ADNOC operates major fields through its own
subsidiaries regardless of equity share, so the operating management system is ADNOC's and
remains stable across changes in partner composition — continuity that suppresses
integration risk but correspondingly limits an incoming partner's operational influence. In
the UK, where operatorship can transfer on a sale, management-system integration is a live
post-completion issue, and the regulator's scrutiny of an incoming party's financial and
technical capability implicitly extends to its competence to run the system.

ADNOC's group sustainability disclosures confirm the alignment of its management systems
with the international standards reviewed above. ADNOC's Group HSE Management System
(GHSEMS), applied uniformly across ADNOC group companies including the operating
subsidiaries that manage upstream joint-venture fields, is structured around a
plan-do-check-act methodology aligned with ISO 45001:2018 (occupational health and safety)
and ISO 14001:2015 (environmental management), underpinned by an enterprise risk
management framework aligned to ISO 31000.[^13] The GHSEMS is implemented through a tiered
architecture of Group Standards, Group Practices, and Group Procedures — a structure that
mirrors the IOGP OMS three-tier model even where IOGP Report 510 is not cited as the
formal documentary basis.[^14] This substantiates the point made above: the
management-system continuity that ADNOC's governance structure delivers is benchmarked
against the same international standards that the IOGP framework prescribes, and the
resulting system-level stability is a confirmable feature of ADNOC's operating model
rather than merely an inference from its state-ownership structure.

## 4A.3 The Capital-Project Governance Lens

The capital-project and megaproject literature explains, more fundamentally than strategic
preference alone, why the JV form dominates upstream. Flyvbjerg, Bruzelius and Rothengatter
document the systematic cost overruns and benefit shortfalls that characterise megaprojects,
driven by optimism bias and strategic misrepresentation in appraisal.[^6] A JV distributes
that downside across partners, which is the structural rationale beneath the risk-sharing
the strategic analysis asserts; it also complements the real-option framing already in the
literature, since a JV stake caps a partner's exposure to an optimism-biased project while
preserving upside. Merrow's empirical work on industrial megaprojects identifies front-end
loading, owner-team capability, and clarity of objectives as the principal determinants of
success.[^7] On that account, JV governance quality — operator capability and the
allocation of decision rights — is itself a success factor, and an M&A transaction that
disrupts operator continuity imports project risk. KPMG's analysis of major capital-project
governance reaches a parallel conclusion at board level, emphasising oversight and
stage-gating;[^8] a JV's operating or management committee is precisely the governance
forum that literature prescribes, and a change of control that weakens it degrades project
assurance.

Two further sources situate appraisal within this frame. HM Treasury's Green Book sets out
an appraisal methodology that mandates explicit correction for optimism bias and structured
sensitivity analysis,[^9] a benchmark of particular relevance where state entities appraise
JV participation; its optimism-bias uplift offers a principled reading of earn-outs and
phased investment as mechanisms for pricing appraisal uncertainty into a deal. No
comparable UAE federal appraisal instrument has been identified: the nearest analogue is
the UAE Ministry of Finance's 2024 *Manual on Public-Private Partnership Projects*, which
covers project appraisal methodology for PPP concessions — initial analysis, technical
feasibility, financial feasibility, and contracting model — but does not mandate the
optimism-bias correction or structured sensitivity analysis that are the Green Book's
substantive contribution.[^15] That absence is analytically significant: where the UK
mandates explicit bias correction as a condition of government appraisal, UAE public-sector
JV participation decisions appear to proceed without an equivalent structural safeguard,
concentrating appraisal-optimism risk in the single state actor who dominates UAE concession
governance. PMBOK's
principles-based approach to project delivery — tailoring, stewardship, and stakeholder
engagement — maps onto the multi-party delivery a JV demands.[^10]

Read together, this literature recasts the "platform and guardrail" duality of the
strategic section. M&A by JV interest transfer preserves the project-governance
architecture — operator, committee, management system — while reallocating ownership, which
is why the JV is both the enabler and the constraint. The comparison again divides on
governance model. The UAE's state-led structure delivers the strong, continuous owner team
Merrow associates with success, at the cost of concentrating appraisal optimism in a single
state actor; the UK's market-led structure distributes governance across partners under
regulatory oversight, gaining flexibility but exposing projects to operator discontinuity on
transfer.

## 4A.4 Trilemma Synthesis

These three substrata bear directly on the trilemma. For **security of supply**, continuity
of operatorship and a robust operating management system safeguard production across
ownership changes, with national-security screening operating as a further layer on top.
For **affordability and economic efficiency**, the accounting classification of an interest
shapes cost of capital and reserve-based borrowing, while project-governance discipline and
Green Book–style appraisal rigour constrain the overruns that erode value and deal flow.
For **sustainability**, ISO 45001 and the IOGP framework embed the safety and environmental
management that responsible transfer requires, and the adequacy of decommissioning
provisioning determines which buyers can credibly assume late-life assets.

The comparative conclusion is that the two jurisdictions optimise different corners of the
trilemma through the same substrate. The UAE privileges security and continuity through
enduring state governance and stable management systems; the UK privileges efficiency and
flexibility through market governance, transferable operatorship, and standardised
management systems under regulatory supervision. It is in the accounting, risk, and
project-governance layer — not in the headline contractual and regulatory rules alone — that
each model's trilemma trade-offs are actually settled.

## Sources — Gap-Closure Status

### §4A.1 — IFRS 11 Classification (Partially Resolved)

ADNOC Gas PLC financial statements confirm equity-method accounting for midstream and
downstream joint ventures (AGP JV, ALNG JV) treated as associates.[^11] Whether upstream
concession interests held by IOC minority partners (Eni, PTTEP, OMV in Ghasha) are
similarly equity-accounted or treated as joint operations turns on the legal form of the
specific concession instrument, which is not publicly available. The analysis in §4A.1 has
been updated to reflect this distinction. **Residual gap:** access to the specific Ghasha
or Habshan concession documentation to confirm incorporated-company vs unincorporated-JOA
structure.

### §4A.2 — ADNOC OMS Alignment (Resolved)

ADNOC's Group HSE Management System (GHSEMS) is confirmed as aligned with ISO 45001:2018,
ISO 14001:2015, and ISO 31000 through ADNOC's 2024 Sustainability Report.[^13] IOGP
Report 510 is not cited as the formal documentary basis, but the three-tier architecture of
the GHSEMS parallels the IOGP OMS framework substantively. §4A.2 updated accordingly.
**No residual gap.**

### §4A.3 — UAE Appraisal Guidance (Resolved: No Equivalent Exists)

No UAE federal appraisal guidance analogous to the Green Book has been identified. The
nearest analogue is the UAE Ministry of Finance *Manual on Public-Private Partnership
Projects* (2024),[^15] which covers PPP project appraisal but lacks optimism-bias
correction and structured sensitivity analysis. This absence has been incorporated into
§4A.3 as a structural UAE–UK difference. **No residual gap.**
