    Norway CCUS/CCS Joint Ventures and Joint Operating Agreements — A Governance Analysis¶

    Caveats: All sources cited below are secondary or publicly available documents. Investment figures and capacity values should be verified against current Gassnova and Northern Lights JV publications before transactional or formally citable use. This document is prepared for PhD research purposes only.

    Overview¶

    Norwegian CCUS is governed through a dedicated, purpose-built regulatory architecture: a specific storage-licensing regime under the Petroleum Act, state-funded infrastructure via the Longship programme, and a tripartite IOC JV (Northern Lights) operating under contractual obligations to Gassnova as the state's counterpart. This stands in direct contrast to the UAE model, where CCUS is embedded inside upstream petroleum concessions with no standalone storage statute (see UAE CCUS governance analysis).

    For the foundational annotated sources on Northern Lights, see Literature: JV Governance — Northern Lights section.

    1. The Norwegian CCS Regulatory Architecture¶

    1.1 Storage Licensing Under the Petroleum Act¶

    Section 4-7 of the Norwegian Petroleum Act (1996, as amended) gives the Ministry of Energy authority to grant licences for the storage of CO₂ in subsea geological formations. This creates a separate storage-licensing layer, conceptually distinct from production licences, with its own terms, conditions, and obligations [1]. The licence holder bears both the operational and the post-closure stewardship obligation — until liability transfers to the Norwegian state.

    The storage-licensing framework aligns Norway with the EU CCS Directive (2009/31/EC), which Norway adopted via the EEA Agreement, and distinguishes the Norwegian model from all current UAE CCUS arrangements, where storage rights derive implicitly from petroleum concession title rather than from a dedicated instrument [2].

    1.2 The Longship Programme and State Funding¶

    Longship (Norwegian: Langskip) is the Norwegian Government's full-scale CCS programme, approved by the Storting (Parliament) in December 2020. The government committed to covering approximately 80% of the capital cost of the Phase 1 infrastructure and approximately 95% of operating costs during an initial period, with the IOC joint-venture partners (Equinor, Shell, TotalEnergies) covering the remainder [3, 4].

    This funding structure has a direct governance implication: state money follows state oversight. Gassnova was mandated as the government's contractual counterpart to the Northern Lights JV, triggering the annual benefits-realisation reporting obligations and experience-transfer requirements that impose transparency standards on the JV well beyond what a purely commercial JOA would require [5].

    1.3 The Regulatory Enabling Environment¶

    Three additional legal instruments support the Northern Lights model:

    • London Protocol / OSPAR Convention amendments — cross-border CO₂ transport requires bilateral agreements under the London Protocol. Norway has completed bilateral frameworks with Sweden, Belgium, Denmark, the Netherlands, and Switzerland [6]. These multilateral governance obligations have no analogue in UAE upstream law.
    • PSA Framework Regulations — the Petroleum Safety Authority's management regulations impose obligations on each JV participant (not only the operator) for HSE management systems, creating direct accountability rather than vicarious operator liability [1].
    • NPD Data Regulations — CO₂ storage operators must submit monitoring and injection data to the NPD, which are eventually published, making the Norwegian CCS data environment among the most transparent globally [1].

    2. Northern Lights JV — Structure and Governance¶

    2.1 JV Formation¶

    The Northern Lights JV was formed by Equinor (33.3%), Shell (33.3%), and TotalEnergies (33.3%) under a Joint Venture Agreement preceded by: (a) a pre-FID study agreement between Gassnova and Equinor as the government's technical partner; and (b) a collaboration agreement among the three IOCs governing study work and providing the template for the JVA [7]. The equal three-way split reflects political balance among the major IOCs engaged on the NCS; operatorship was awarded to Equinor as the state's majority-owned NOC.

    2.2 Open-Source Infrastructure Model¶

    Northern Lights operates as open-source transport and storage infrastructure: it accepts CO₂ from third-party industrial emitters (not only from the JV partners' operations) and provides transport and storage services on regulated terms. The first confirmed customer is Heidelberg Materials (cement); Yara (fertilisers) and other industrial emitters have signed heads of agreement [8].

    This open-source design has no equivalent in UAE CCUS practice. Al Reyadah captures CO₂ from a single dedicated industrial source (Emirates Steel) and injects it into ADNOC-operated fields for EOR. Habshan captures CO₂ from ADNOC Gas's own processing operations. Neither project offers third-party access on open terms. The Northern Lights model represents a public-goods approach to CCS infrastructure; the UAE model is vertically integrated within the ADNOC group.

    2.3 Capacity and Investment¶

    Phase 1 provides 1.5 million tonnes per annum (Mtpa) capacity, with first commercial injection commenced in 2025. Phase 2 FID was taken in March 2025, expanding to at least 5 Mtpa at a total investment of NOK 7.5 billion [8]. The Phase 2 expansion is commercially driven: revenue from storage contracts with multiple emitters provides the economic case for scale-up, in contrast to Phase 1, which was primarily state-funded.

    2.4 Contractual Accountability to Gassnova¶

    Two contract types sit above the standard JVA and define the Norwegian model's distinctive governance layer:

    • State Aid Agreement — sets out the conditions under which the Norwegian Government provides funding, including performance milestones, audit rights, and the mechanism by which state funding is drawn down. Gassnova administers compliance [5].
    • Benefits-Realisation and Experience-Transfer Contracts — require the JV to report annually on whether state investment objectives are being met (benefits-realisation report) and to share operational experience with Gassnova and the wider CCS community (experience-transfer report). These documents are published, creating a public record of JV performance [5, 9].

    Neither instrument type exists in UAE upstream JV practice, where concession terms are confidential and state oversight is exercised through ADNOC's controlling interest rather than through contractual transparency obligations imposed on the JV.

    3. How Norway's CCS JOA Handles the Governance Gap¶

    The four structural problems that UAE CCUS JOAs face (identified in the companion analysis) are directly addressed by Norwegian law and the Northern Lights contractual architecture.

    3.1 Long-Tail Liability — Codified State Transfer¶

    Norwegian CCS regulations provide that residual long-term liability for injected CO₂ transfers to the Norwegian state — not to the JV participants — upon satisfaction of defined conditions (typically a post-closure monitoring period during which no anomalous CO₂ migration is detected) [3, 10]. This liability transfer is statutory, not merely contractual, meaning it cannot be reversed by private agreement and survives the winding up of the JV. The state accepts this liability in exchange for taking the storage-right revenue during operations.

    The UAE has no equivalent codified mechanism; residual CCUS stewardship risk sits inside the JOA indefinitely or reverts to ADNOC as sovereign concession-holder without a defined transfer pathway.

    3.2 Pore Space and Storage Tenure — Separate Licence¶

    The storage licence under Section 4-7 of the Petroleum Act creates a distinct storage interest, separate from any production licence. The Northern Lights JV holds the storage licence, and the interests in that licence are defined separately from any hydrocarbon production interests. This cleanly resolves the pore-space question that UAE JOAs cannot easily address: the "storage interest" is a named legal concept with its own percentage allocations, governance rights, and exit mechanics [1].

    3.3 MRV and Financial Assurance — Regulatory Mandate¶

    Operators under the Norwegian CCS framework are required to maintain a monitoring plan approved by the NPD, submit monitoring data, and demonstrate financial assurance for post-closure obligations before the state will accept liability transfer [6]. These obligations are not left to JOA negotiation — they are conditions of the storage licence. The cash-call mechanism within the Northern Lights JVA therefore funds monitoring obligations as a first-class capex category, not an afterthought.

    3.4 State Oversight as Governance Substitute¶

    Where the UAE relies on ADNOC's majority interest to discipline JV governance, Norway uses three parallel oversight mechanisms: (a) PSA regulatory inspection; (b) Gassnova contractual oversight; and (c) NPD data submission requirements. The effect is that JV governance in Norway is simultaneously contractual (JVA), regulatory (PSA/NPD), and quasi-public (Gassnova state-aid conditions) — a layered architecture that reduces information asymmetry between the state and the JV at every level.

    4. Conclusion and Comparative Thesis¶

    The Northern Lights JV represents the most developed example in the world of what a purpose-built CCS JV governance framework looks like when the host state commits to full-stack design: dedicated storage licence, state-funded infrastructure, contractual transparency obligations, codified liability-transfer to the state, and open-source third-party access.

    The Norway–UAE comparison reveals not a spectrum but a categorical difference:

    Dimension Norway (Northern Lights) UAE (Habshan/Ghasha)
    Storage legal basis Dedicated Section 4-7 licence Emirate concession title
    Regulatory oversight PSA + NPD + Gassnova ADNOC majority interest
    Liability transfer Statutory transfer to Norwegian state No codified mechanism
    Transparency Published annual reports, NPD data Concession terms confidential
    Access model Open-source T&S infrastructure Vertically integrated within ADNOC
    State funding 80% Phase 1 capex / 95% opex No dedicated public fund

    This categorical difference has implications for the energy-trilemma sustainability dimension: the Norwegian model internalises the post-closure social cost of CO₂ storage within the state, removing it from private JV balance sheets. The UAE model leaves that cost allocation unresolved inside petroleum JOAs not designed for multi-generational obligations — which is either a prudent deferral of complex legal design, or a material governance gap that will become apparent as CCUS capacity scales toward the 10 Mtpa target.

    The thesis argues the latter, and uses this structural comparison to contribute to the literature on how JV governance mediates national energy-trilemma trajectories.
