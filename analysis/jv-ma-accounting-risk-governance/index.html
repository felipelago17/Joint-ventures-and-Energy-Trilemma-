    4A. Accounting, Risk, and Project-Governance Foundations of JV-Based Oil M&A¶

    The preceding section established why firms route oil and gas M&A through joint-venture (JV) interest transfers rather than outright asset sales. That strategic account, however, rests on three substrata it does not itself examine: how JV interests are recognised in the accounts, what operational risk and management-system obligations travel with them, and how the wider capital-project governance literature explains the dominance of the JV form. This section supplies that substrate and links each dimension to the energy trilemma of security, affordability, and sustainability that frames this research.

    4A.1 Accounting Classification and the Valuation of JV Interests¶

    The financing constraints flagged earlier — reserve-based lenders discounting interests that convey a share of production rather than reserve ownership, and the resulting valuation gaps — have a concrete accounting mechanism in IFRS 11. The standard classifies a joint arrangement by the parties' rights and obligations rather than its legal form: a joint operation confers direct rights to assets and obligations for liabilities, recognised line-by-line in proportion to the interest held, whereas a joint venture confers rights only to the net assets of a separate vehicle and is reported through the equity method as a single net-investment line.1 The distinction is decisive for an acquirer. A buyer of a joint-operation interest brings its share of reserves, production assets, and decommissioning liabilities directly onto its own balance sheet, with the corresponding effect on gearing and on the asset base available as collateral. A buyer of an equity-accounted JV interest consolidates none of this; it reports only a net investment, leaving lenders with weaker visibility of the underlying reserves against which reserve-based lending is advanced. This is the accounting source of the higher cost of capital and the valuation gaps the strategic analysis attributes to concession structures.

    The treatment of pre-production expenditure compounds the point. IFRS 6 permits entities to capitalise exploration and evaluation outlay under an elected accounting policy, subject to impairment testing once technical feasibility and commercial viability are assessed.2 In farm-in transactions — the staged transfers central to the strategic analysis of the UK Continental Shelf — the carrying value of capitalised exploration and evaluation assets, and whether a farminee's carry of costs is itself capitalised, directly shape both the consideration and the design of any earn-out. Accounting policy, in other words, is not a back-office matter but a determinant of how a JV-based deal is priced.

    Both jurisdictions operate within the IFRS framework: UK-listed independents and the ADNOC-listed entities alike report under IFRS, so the standards converge even where the structures diverge. UK unincorporated joint operating agreements tend toward joint-operation accounting, while interests held through incorporated operating companies — the more common UAE pattern, in which ADNOC retains a majority and the international partner a minority — are more likely to fall to equity accounting for that minority. The precise classification turns on the specific rights and obligations each contract confers and cannot be assumed from form alone; the determination for ADNOC concession interests requires a primary source the supplied literature does not resolve (see Sources to add).

    4A.2 Risk and HSE Management Systems as Integration and Due-Diligence Factors¶

    Where the host paper observes that due diligence must now reach beyond legal compliance, the operational counterpart of that claim lies in the management-system standards an incoming partner or operator inherits. Three are material. ISO 31000 sets out the principles, framework, and process for enterprise risk management; though not a certifiable standard, it is the benchmark against which a target's risk maturity is assessed.3 ISO 45001 establishes a certifiable occupational health and safety management system whose obligations — and exposures — transfer with operatorship, a consideration that becomes acute in late-life and decommissioning assets where safety liability is concentrated.4 The upstream-specific architecture is provided by the IOGP Operating Management System framework (Report 510), widely adopted across the sector to integrate leadership, risk, and asset-integrity controls into a single operating system.5

    These standards reframe the operatorship clauses examined in the next section. Because the operating management system and the HSE regime travel with operatorship, a buyer assuming the operator role assumes not only contractual control but the asset-integrity and safety obligations the management system encodes; a buyer remaining a non-operator must instead satisfy itself that the incumbent operator's system is robust. Management-system maturity therefore becomes a due-diligence object in its own right, distinct from title and from the ESG screening discussed later.

    The comparative contrast is sharp. In the UAE, ADNOC operates major fields through its own subsidiaries regardless of equity share, so the operating management system is ADNOC's and remains stable across changes in partner composition — continuity that suppresses integration risk but correspondingly limits an incoming partner's operational influence. In the UK, where operatorship can transfer on a sale, management-system integration is a live post-completion issue, and the regulator's scrutiny of an incoming party's financial and technical capability implicitly extends to its competence to run the system.

    4A.3 The Capital-Project Governance Lens¶

    The capital-project and megaproject literature explains, more fundamentally than strategic preference alone, why the JV form dominates upstream. Flyvbjerg, Bruzelius and Rothengatter document the systematic cost overruns and benefit shortfalls that characterise megaprojects, driven by optimism bias and strategic misrepresentation in appraisal.6 A JV distributes that downside across partners, which is the structural rationale beneath the risk-sharing the strategic analysis asserts; it also complements the real-option framing already in the literature, since a JV stake caps a partner's exposure to an optimism-biased project while preserving upside. Merrow's empirical work on industrial megaprojects identifies front-end loading, owner-team capability, and clarity of objectives as the principal determinants of success.7 On that account, JV governance quality — operator capability and the allocation of decision rights — is itself a success factor, and an M&A transaction that disrupts operator continuity imports project risk. KPMG's analysis of major capital-project governance reaches a parallel conclusion at board level, emphasising oversight and stage-gating;8 a JV's operating or management committee is precisely the governance forum that literature prescribes, and a change of control that weakens it degrades project assurance.

    Two further sources situate appraisal within this frame. HM Treasury's Green Book sets out an appraisal methodology that mandates explicit correction for optimism bias and structured sensitivity analysis,9 a benchmark of particular relevance where state entities appraise JV participation; its optimism-bias uplift offers a principled reading of earn-outs and phased investment as mechanisms for pricing appraisal uncertainty into a deal. PMBOK's principles-based approach to project delivery — tailoring, stewardship, and stakeholder engagement — maps onto the multi-party delivery a JV demands.10

    Read together, this literature recasts the "platform and guardrail" duality of the strategic section. M&A by JV interest transfer preserves the project-governance architecture — operator, committee, management system — while reallocating ownership, which is why the JV is both the enabler and the constraint. The comparison again divides on governance model. The UAE's state-led structure delivers the strong, continuous owner team Merrow associates with success, at the cost of concentrating appraisal optimism in a single state actor; the UK's market-led structure distributes governance across partners under regulatory oversight, gaining flexibility but exposing projects to operator discontinuity on transfer.

    4A.4 Trilemma Synthesis¶

    These three substrata bear directly on the trilemma. For security of supply, continuity of operatorship and a robust operating management system safeguard production across ownership changes, with national-security screening operating as a further layer on top. For affordability and economic efficiency, the accounting classification of an interest shapes cost of capital and reserve-based borrowing, while project-governance discipline and Green Book–style appraisal rigour constrain the overruns that erode value and deal flow. For sustainability, ISO 45001 and the IOGP framework embed the safety and environmental management that responsible transfer requires, and the adequacy of decommissioning provisioning determines which buyers can credibly assume late-life assets.

    The comparative conclusion is that the two jurisdictions optimise different corners of the trilemma through the same substrate. The UAE privileges security and continuity through enduring state governance and stable management systems; the UK privileges efficiency and flexibility through market governance, transferable operatorship, and standardised management systems under regulatory supervision. It is in the accounting, risk, and project-governance layer — not in the headline contractual and regulatory rules alone — that each model's trilemma trade-offs are actually settled.
