    UAE Joint Ventures and Joint Operating Agreements in CCUS/CCS — A Governance Analysis¶

    Caveats: All sources cited below are secondary or web-based. Participating-interest figures must be confirmed against primary concession filings. The UAE Climate Law and relevant concession terms should be read in the original before any transactional or citable use. This document is prepared for PhD research purposes only.

    Overview¶

    UAE CCUS is structured inside the existing upstream concession architecture rather than through dedicated standalone storage ventures. There is no bespoke CCS storage statute; pore space and storage rights derive from the same emirate-level sovereign title as hydrocarbons. The JV/JOA instruments governing CCUS are therefore largely petroleum instruments carrying obligations they were not drafted to bear.

    For the contrasting Norwegian model see Literature: JV Governance — Northern Lights.

    1. No Standalone CCS Regime¶

    CCUS governance in the UAE operates across three overlapping regulatory layers.

    Emirate-level petroleum and concession architecture. Under Article 23 of the UAE Constitution, natural resources are the public property of each emirate, which holds title to oil, gas, and mineral rights [21]. Storage rights flow from sovereign title and are not separately licensed as they are under the EU CCS Directive or the UK storage-licensing regime [24]. CCUS ventures are therefore embedded in upstream concession JVs rather than constituted as standalone storage entities — the defining structural contrast with Norway's Northern Lights and the UK's cluster-based track-1 projects [22].

    Federal UAE Climate Law. CCUS is a listed compliance measure under the federal climate framework. Scope 1 and Scope 2 greenhouse-gas reporting became mandatory from 2026, with Scope 3 reporting anticipated from 2027 [26]. This creates a top-down disclosure obligation that sits above, but does not substitute for, concession-level governance of storage activities.

    Abu Dhabi MRV via the Environment Agency Abu Dhabi (EAD). Abu Dhabi's Enhanced Transparency Framework links facility-level monitoring, reporting, and verification to the national platform [23]. As with the federal climate law, this creates a reporting layer but leaves the underlying liability and stewardship questions to contractual arrangements within the JOA.

    The consequence is that CCUS in the UAE is primarily a concession matter, not a dedicated storage-licensing matter — a structural choice with material implications for long-tail liability allocation [27, 28].

    2. Project Landscape and JV/JOA Arrangements¶

    2.1 Al Reyadah (2016) — Incubation Template¶

    Al Reyadah was the Middle East's first commercial-scale CCUS facility and the world's first for the iron and steel sector [6, 12]. The JV was constituted by a 2013 JV agreement between ADNOC (51%) and Masdar (49%), with first injection in 2016. It captures up to 800,000 tonnes per annum (tpa) of CO₂ from Emirates Steel, transported approximately 43–50 km to the Rumaitha and Bab fields for enhanced oil recovery (EOR) [7, 8].

    ADNOC acquired Masdar's 49% in 2018, integrating Al Reyadah into ADNOC Onshore [9, 10]. The storage leg sat inside an existing upstream JOA, making this the first documented instance of CCS obligation absorption into a UAE petroleum JOA. The buyout compressed the governance complexity but did not resolve the question of what happens to post-injection obligations when a two-party JV winds down through acquisition [11].

    2.2 Habshan CCUS (FID September 2023) — Intra-Group Operating Mandate¶

    The Habshan facility was sanctioned in September 2023, with ADNOC committing to one of the largest integrated carbon capture projects in the MENA region [1]. ADNOC Gas was awarded the operating mandate on behalf of ADNOC, covering capture units, pipeline, and injection wells [2]. The engineering, procurement, and construction (EPC) contract (approximately USD 615 million) was awarded to Petrofac, and the capture technology licence was granted by Shell under its Cansolv process [3, 4, 5].

    At 1.5 million tpa (mtpa), Habshan approximately triples Abu Dhabi's CCS capacity to 2.3 mtpa, with completion targeted for 2026 [4]. The governance structure is an intra-group operating mandate with a third-party technology licence and EPC — not a classic multi-party JOA [2, 3]. There is no IOC co-investor carrying a participating interest in the storage infrastructure; ADNOC bears all storage-obligation risk internally.

    2.3 Hail and Ghasha — Multi-Party International JV¶

    The Hail and Ghasha concession is a 40-year ultra-sour gas project designed from the outset to operate at net-zero CO₂, incorporating 1.5 mtpa of capture [13, 14]. In February 2026, ADNOC signed up-to-USD 11 billion of structured financing with its concession partners to monetise midstream future gas production [19].

    Indicative participating interests (rotate over the concession life — confirm against primary filings; see also data/ghasha-participating-interests.csv):

    Participant Interest Role / Note
    ADNOC (operator) 55% Controlling; below ADNOC's usual 60%, reflecting cost and complexity
    Eni 25% Co-financing partner in 2026 structured financing
    PTTEP 10% Acquired from Wintershall Dea [17]
    OMV 5% Entered via concession agreement (40-yr term, November 2018) [15, 16]
    Lukoil 5% Russian partner

    ADNOC's headline target is to double CCS capacity to 10 mtpa by 2030, within a Net Zero by 2045 objective [20].

    3. Why CCS JOAs Are Not Upstream JOAs — The Governance Gap¶

    A standard AIPN/AIEN-style JOA was designed for hydrocarbon extraction. Four structural risk-allocation problems arise when CCS obligations are embedded in such an instrument.

    3.1 Long-Tail Liability¶

    CCS operations span three phases: (a) injection and operations; (b) post-injection site care (typically 10–50+ years); and (c) long-term stewardship, which may extend beyond the concession term itself. Standard JOA exit, default, and decommissioning provisions address the end of hydrocarbon production; they do not address liabilities that persist after the JV winds up [27, 28]. The Ghasha concession term is 40 years — a structure that may be insufficient to contain the post-closure obligation horizon [13].

    3.2 Liability Transfer to the State¶

    Mature CCS jurisdictions — Norway, the UK, the EU — have codified mechanisms by which residual long-term liability transfers to a state entity or stewardship fund upon satisfaction of conditions (typically a defined monitoring period with no anomalies detected). The UAE has no equivalent codified mechanism [22, 24]. In the absence of a statutory transfer pathway, the JOA must allocate residual stewardship risk among the parties contractually — a burden that falls materially differently on a 5% IOC partner (e.g. OMV or Lukoil) compared with a 55% sovereign operator.

    3.3 Pore Space and Storage Tenure¶

    Because storage is not separately licensed under UAE law, the JOA cannot cleanly define a "storage interest" distinct from the production interest [22, 24]. Whether the percentage interests governing hydrocarbon-economics cost-sharing should also govern CO₂ stewardship cost-sharing is unresolved. This creates a potential conflict between the economic logic of the production JOA and the physical and temporal logic of CO₂ storage [24, 25].

    3.4 MRV and Financial Assurance¶

    In mature regimes, operators must hold financial assurance (a bond or equivalent) for post-closure monitoring and remediation. The cash-call and default provisions that petroleum JOAs use for drilling and production capex were not designed to fund monitoring obligations measured in decades. The scale mismatch — drilling decisions take months, storage-stewardship commitments take generations — requires provisions that must be drafted expressly rather than inferred from existing JOA machinery [25, 27, 28].

    4. Conclusion¶

    The UAE model can be characterised as concession-embedded CCUS: capture-led, EOR/storage-integrated, governed via upstream JVs (Hail and Ghasha) or intra-group mandates (Habshan, Al Reyadah post-2018), resting on emirate sovereign title.

    The model is commercially sophisticated and demonstrably bankable — the USD 11 billion structured Ghasha financing confirms that IOC co-investors accept UAE concession-JV terms as a governance framework for long-duration capital deployment [19]. The structural deficit, however, is the absence of a codified long-term liability-transfer and stewardship regime. This gap is currently managed inside JOAs that were not designed for multi-decade post-closure obligations.

    This is the seam where comparative JV-governance analysis has the most to contribute. The absence of a state stewardship vehicle comparable to Gassnova, the absence of a dedicated storage licence comparable to Norwegian and UK frameworks, and the absence of open-source T&S infrastructure comparable to Northern Lights all reflect a regulatory design choice — implicit rather than explicit — that the sovereign concession-holder (ADNOC/Abu Dhabi) will absorb residual stewardship risk rather than transferring it to a public institution. The question of who bears enduring risk over a sovereign-linked, multi-generational asset — and how that risk allocation maps onto the energy trilemma's sustainability pillar and the computational-sovereignty framing of AI/data governance as national asset stewardship — is the central analytical contribution of this module.
