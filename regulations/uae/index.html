    UAE — Annotated Source Register (CCS/CCUS JV & JOA Governance)¶

    Primary instruments and institutions bearing on CO₂ capture, utilisation and storage in the UAE (Abu Dhabi focus), annotated for relevance to JV/JOA governance and the four-gap framework used in analysis/uae-ccus-jv-joa-governance.md.

    Verification note: The federal instruments are citable from official sources (uaelegislation.gov.ae). The concession-level instruments are largely contractual and confidential — entries flag what is publicly known and mark where primary terms cannot be verified.

    Cross-reference (Norway): For the paired statutory-tenure register see regulations/norway/. For the paired index see regulations/README.md.

    Structural Note¶

    The UAE has no dedicated CCS/CO₂-storage statute. Storage rights are not severable from the upstream estate: they flow from emirate sovereign title over natural resources and are exercised through petroleum concessions and the JOAs beneath them. The four governance gaps (storage tenure, post-closure / long-term liability transfer, pollution liability, MRV) are therefore allocated by contract (JOA) rather than by statute — the inverse of the Norwegian position.

    A. Constitutional and Ownership Foundation¶

    A1. UAE Constitution — Article 23¶

    • Citation: Constitution of the United Arab Emirates (1971, as amended), Article 23.
    • Source: https://u.ae/en/information-and-services/justice-safety-and-the-law/the-constitution-of-the-uae
    • Substance: "Natural resources and wealth in each Emirate shall be considered the public property of that Emirate"; each emirate holds title to its oil reserves and mineral rights.
    • Bearing on the gaps: Structural root of concession-embedded CCUS. Because title (and hence pore space/storage rights) vests in the emirate and is licensed contractually rather than through a separate storage estate, the JOA cannot cleanly define a storage interest distinct from the production interest — leaving tenure and stewardship to negotiation.

    B. Federal Climate Framework¶

    B1. UAE Climate Law 2024¶

    • Citation: Federal Decree-Law No. (11) of 2024 on the Reduction of Climate Change Effects ("UAE Climate Law" / "Emirates Climate Law").
    • Dates: Issued 28 August 2024; in force 30 May 2025.
    • Source: https://uaelegislation.gov.ae/en/legislations/2558
    • Scope: Applies to all public and private "sources" in the State, including free zones. First binding national climate framework in MENA; aligned with the UAE Net Zero by 2050 Strategic Initiative and the Paris Agreement.
    • Key features:
    • Mandatory GHG measurement, reporting and verification per Ministry of Climate Change and Environment (MOCCAE) methodologies.
    • CCUS expressly listed among the means by which a source may discharge its emissions-reduction obligation.
    • Administrative/financial penalties (Articles 17–20) ranging AED 50,000–2,000,000, with doubling for repeat violations within two years.
    • Bearing on the gaps: Supplies a federal MRV/compliance driver for CCUS but does not create storage tenure, a long-term-liability-transfer mechanism, or a stewardship-fund regime. It motivates capture/storage without resolving the post-closure governance gap.

    B2. National Carbon Credit Registry¶

    • Citation: UAE Cabinet Decision No. 67 of 2024 concerning the National Carbon Credit Registry.
    • Relevance: Companion to B1; mandatory participation in emissions-tracking/carbon-market mechanisms; sets programme thresholds, verification procedures and carbon-credit eligibility.
    • Operational tool: Integrated Emissions Quantification Tool (MOCCAE, introduced October 2025) — digital MRV platform for Scope 1–3 boundary mapping and reporting.

    C. Reporting and MRV (Emirate Level)¶

    C1. Abu Dhabi Enhanced Transparency Framework¶

    • Body: Environment Agency — Abu Dhabi (EAD).
    • URL: https://www.ead.gov.ae
    • Relevance: Emirate-level MRV system linking to the national reporting platform; the practical reporting layer for Abu Dhabi CCUS sources, operating alongside federal Climate Law obligations (B1).

    D. Concession Framework (Contractual Layer)¶

    D1. Abu Dhabi Petroleum Concession Framework¶

    • Nature: Emirate-level, contractual. There is no single published "concession law" creating a storage estate; rights are conferred through individual concession agreements granted by Abu Dhabi (historically via the Supreme Petroleum Council / ADNOC governance structure).
    • Worked example — Ghasha concession: 40-year term (from November 2018); hosts the Hail & Ghasha development designed for net-zero CO₂ with ~1.5 Mtpa capture. Participating interests held by ADNOC (operator) and international partners — see data/ghasha-participating-interests.csv.
    • Bearing on the gaps: Where all four governance gaps land in the UAE model — storage tenure, post-closure liability, financial assurance and MRV cost-sharing are matters for the concession/JOA, not statute.
    • Verification note: Concession and JOA terms are confidential/commercial and not publicly available; do not assert specific clause-level allocations without sight of the primary agreements. Participating interests rotate over time — confirm against current filings.

    D2. Intra-Group Operating Mandate — Worked Example¶

    • Instrument type: Operating-on-behalf-of arrangement (not a multi-party JOA).
    • Example — Habshan CCUS: Built, operated and maintained by ADNOC Gas on behalf of ADNOC; 1.5 Mtpa; technology licence (Shell Cansolv) and EPC (Petrofac). Illustrates that some UAE CCUS sits under corporate operating mandates rather than concession JOAs.

    D3. Al Reyadah JV — Historical CCS Concession Structure¶

    First commercial-scale CCUS project in MENA (iron and steel sector), commissioned November 2016. CO₂ captured from Emirates Steel Industries (ESI), Mussafah → ~45 km pipeline → ADNOC onshore EOR fields.

    Cross-reference (Norway): For the Northern Lights comparative case see regulations/norway/README.md § C.

    E. Gap Entry — Explicit Absence¶

    E1. No Dedicated CCS Storage Statute¶

    • Status: No UAE equivalent to Norway's CO₂ Storage Regulations 2014 — there is no separate storage tenure, no statutory cessation/closure framework, no statutory liability transfer to the State, and no fisheries-compensation regime.
    • Consequence: Long-tail stewardship that may outlast a 40-year concession term has no codified statutory home; it must be solved inside JOAs not designed for multi-decade post-closure obligations — the central finding of the comparative analysis in analysis/uae-ccus-jv-joa-governance.md.
