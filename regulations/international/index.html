    International — Annotated Source Register¶

    Multilateral treaties, model contracts, and frameworks relevant to upstream joint ventures, CCS/CCUS governance, and the energy trilemma.

    Cross-reference: For national-level registers see regulations/norway/ and regulations/uae/. For the four-gap comparison index see regulations/README.md.

    A. Marine and Offshore Framework¶

    A1. United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS) 1982¶

    • Citation: United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, 10 December 1982, 1833 UNTS 3 (in force 16 November 1994).
    • URL: https://www.un.org/depts/los/convention_agreements/texts/unclos/unclos_e.pdf
    • Key provisions for JV governance:
    • Part V (EEZ) / Part VI (Continental Shelf): Coastal-state sovereign rights over exploration and exploitation of natural resources on the continental shelf, including subsoil — the international law basis for both Norway's NCS licensing jurisdiction and Abu Dhabi's emirate concession authority.
    • Art. 82 (Continental Shelf beyond 200 nm): Payments-in-kind or contributions for exploitation of non-living resources beyond 200 nm; relevant for ultra-deepwater JV cost structures.
    • Part XII (Marine Environment Protection): Obligations to prevent, reduce and control marine pollution; applicable to offshore CO₂ pipeline leaks from CCS T&S operations; underpins the environmental-permitting baseline for Northern Lights.
    • Part XIII (Marine Scientific Research): Research-permit requirements relevant to storage-site characterisation surveys.
    • Bearing on CCS: Norway's EL001 Aurora licence and the Northern Lights subsea pipeline operate within the continental shelf jurisdiction created by Part VI. The London Protocol (A2) addresses CO₂ disposal specifically.

    A2. London Protocol — 1996 Protocol to the London Convention; 2009/2013 Amendments¶

    • Citation (base instrument): 1996 Protocol to the Convention on the Prevention of Marine Pollution by Dumping of Wastes and Other Matter, 7 November 1996 (in force 24 March 2006). IMO.
    • 2009 Amendment: Resolution LP.3(4) of 30 October 2009 — amended Art. 6 to allow the export of CO₂ streams for the purpose of disposal at sea (into sub-seabed geological formations), permitting bilateral/multilateral cross-border CO₂ transport.
    • 2013 Amendment (not yet in force as of 2026): Resolution LP.4(8) of 4 October 2013 — Article 6bis; pending entry into force; interim application by agreement.
    • URL: https://www.imo.org/en/OurWork/Environment/Pages/London-Convention-Protocol.aspx
    • Bearing on CCS: The single most important international legal instrument for cross-border CO₂ transport. Without the 2009/2013 amendments, export of CO₂ from one country for injection into another's continental shelf would be prohibited as "dumping." Norway has completed bilateral interim-application agreements with Sweden, Belgium, Denmark, the Netherlands, and Switzerland to enable Northern Lights Phase 2 cross-border shipments pending Art. 6bis entering into force. The UAE has no equivalent cross-border CO₂ transport arrangement.

    B. Investment Protection¶

    B1. Energy Charter Treaty (ECT) 1994¶

    • Citation: Energy Charter Treaty, 17 December 1994, 2080 UNTS 95 (in force 16 April 1998).
    • URL: https://www.energycharter.org/process/energy-charter-treaty-1994/energy-charter-treaty/
    • Key provisions:
    • Part III (Investment Promotion and Protection): Fair and equitable treatment; prohibition of unreasonable impairment; national treatment; most-favoured-nation treatment; full protection and security — applicable to IOC investments in JVs in Contracting Party territory.
    • Art. 26 (Dispute Settlement): Investor-state arbitration before ICSID, SCC, or ad hoc tribunal — the primary international remedy for IOC JV partners against host-state expropriation or regulatory taking.
    • Part IV (Miscellaneous): Transit provisions.
    • Relevance to Norway/UAE:
    • Norway is a Contracting Party; its NCS licensing and SDFI structure must be consistent with ECT obligations (Norway has maintained consistency through the NPD licensing framework's non-discriminatory access provisions).
    • UAE is not a Contracting Party to the ECT, meaning IOC JV partners in UAE concessions cannot invoke Art. 26 investment arbitration. IOC investment protection in UAE concessions relies on bilateral investment treaties (BITs) and the concession's own dispute-resolution clause.
    • Status note: Multiple EU member states have withdrawn or are withdrawing following Achmea (C-284/16) and the ECT modernisation controversy (2022); the treaty's future applicability to intra-EU renewable-energy JVs is contested.

    C. Climate and Sustainability¶

    C1. Paris Agreement 2015¶

    • Citation: Paris Agreement under the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, 12 December 2015, TIAS 16-1104 (in force 4 November 2016). UNFCCC.
    • URL: https://unfccc.int/process-and-meetings/the-paris-agreement
    • Key provisions:
    • Art. 2: Holding global average temperature increase to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels; pursuing 1.5°C.
    • Art. 4 (NDCs): Each Party to communicate and maintain successive Nationally Determined Contributions ratcheting up over time; Norway and UAE each have NDCs listing CCUS as a mitigation measure.
    • Art. 6 (Carbon Markets): Cooperative approaches for internationally transferred mitigation outcomes (ITMOs); basis for potential cross-border carbon crediting of Northern Lights storage services.
    • Bearing on JV governance: The NDC ratchet mechanism creates the regulatory driver pushing both Norway and the UAE toward CCUS investment. JOA ESG clauses in the 2023 AIEN Model JOA (C3 below) are calibrated to the Paris Agreement framework. The UAE Climate Law 2024 (regulations/uae/) implements UAE NDC obligations domestically.

    C2. EU CCS Directive 2009/31/EC¶

    • Citation: Directive 2009/31/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 23 April 2009 on the geological storage of carbon dioxide, OJ L 140/114.
    • URL: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32009L0031
    • EEA relevance: Incorporated into the EEA Agreement (Annex XX, Chapter III, No. 21); implemented in Norway via the CO₂ Storage Regulations 2014 (Regs No. 1517).
    • Key provisions:
    • Art. 6 (Storage Permit): Grant, review, update, and withdrawal of storage permits — the EU model for the separate storage-licensing estate that Norway adopts.
    • Art. 18 (Liability Transfer): Post-closure liability transfers to the Competent Authority after a minimum period, on conditions; the statutory model replicated in Norway's Ch 7 CO₂ Storage Regulations — and absent in the UAE.
    • Art. 19 (Financial Security): Pre-closure financial security requirements; post-transfer compensation mechanism.
    • Bearing on Norway/UAE comparison: The Directive is the international statutory template that underpins Norway's CCS governance gap resolutions (storage tenure, liability transfer, MRV, financial security) and has no UAE equivalent. See references/primary-instruments.bib entry eu_ccs_directive_2009.

    D. Model JV/JOA Contracts¶

    D1. AIEN 2023 Model International Joint Operating Agreement¶

    • Citation: Association of International Energy Negotiators (AIEN, formerly AIPN), Model International Joint Operating Agreement, 2023 revision.
    • URL: https://www.aien.org/forms/store/ProductFormPublic/joint-operating-agreement-2023 (commercial; TOC open access)
    • TOC: https://www.aien.org/forms/uploadFiles/A844E00000031.toc.TOC_AIEN_Model_Joint_Operating_Agreement_2023.pdf
    • Status: Supersedes the 2012 AIPN Model JOA; designed to work alongside the 2020 Unitisation and Unit Operating Agreement (UUOA).
    • Key new provisions (2023):
    • Optional GHG emissions clauses — operator duty to limit/mitigate and report periodically; more naturally adopted in Norway (CO₂ tax ~USD 70/tCO₂) than UAE (no equivalent tax internalisation mechanism).
    • Human rights obligations — anchored to UDHR, ILO Declaration, and UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights.
    • Sanctions article — removal of sanctioned operator; withdrawal right for non-sanctioned parties; relevant to Lukoil's 5% Ghasha interest.
    • Decommissioning — non-operators can compel cost estimates; independent expert mechanism; Legacy Wells provisions.
    • Legacy CCS note: The 2023 AIEN JOA does not contain dedicated CCS storage governance provisions (separate storage tenure, long-tail liability, post-closure MRV cost-sharing). This is the model contract analogue of the statutory gap identified for the UAE.
    • Cross-reference: Full annotation in literature/by-theme/jv-governance.md.

    D2. NOROG Model JOA (Norwegian Continental Shelf)¶

    • Citation: Norwegian Oil and Gas Association (NOROG, formerly Norwegian Oil and Gas), Model Joint Operating Agreement for the Norwegian Continental Shelf, current revision.
    • URL: https://www.norskoljeoggass.no/en/publications/
    • Relevance: Standard-form JOA template for all NCS production licences; drafted within the Norwegian regulatory framework (PSA/Sodir oversight; CO₂ tax; NPD data obligations). Incorporates Norwegian law requirements by default — including CO₂ reporting and HSE management obligations that exceed the AIEN baseline. The NOROG Model JOA is the contractual layer sitting beneath Norway's statutory CCS regime; the UAE has no published equivalent standard form.

    E. Production-Sharing and Host-State Frameworks¶

    E1. OPEC Statute 1960 (as amended)¶

    • Citation: Statute of the Organisation of the Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC), as amended through the 15th Amendment (1965, most recently revised). OPEC Secretariat, Vienna.
    • URL: https://www.opec.org/opec_web/static_files_project/media/downloads/publications/OPEC_Statute.pdf
    • Relevance: Production quota system affects JV economics and host-state revenue expectations in all OPEC member states. The UAE (Abu Dhabi) is an OPEC member; Hail & Ghasha gas production is not directly subject to oil quotas but ADNOC's overall upstream strategy is shaped by OPEC coordination. Norway is not an OPEC member; NCS production decisions are commercially driven within the licensing framework.

    E2. IEA Agreement on International Energy Program 1974¶

    • Citation: Agreement on an International Energy Program, 18 November 1974, TIAS 8278 (in force 19 January 1976). International Energy Agency.
    • URL: https://www.iea.org/about/history
    • Relevance: Founding instrument of the IEA; emergency oil-sharing mechanism underpins the energy-security pillar of the trilemma for IEA member states. Norway is an IEA member (associate, then full); UAE is not. The energy-security policy frameworks available to Norway (coordinated IEA response, strategic reserves) therefore differ structurally from those available to the UAE — a governance dimension relevant to JV risk allocation for force-majeure and supply-disruption scenarios.
