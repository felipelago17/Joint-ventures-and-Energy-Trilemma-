# Literature: Contribution of Joint Ventures to the Energy Trilemma

Annotated reading list on how joint-venture structures in the upstream petroleum sector
contribute to — or create tensions within — the three pillars of the WEC energy trilemma:
energy security, energy equity (affordability/access), and environmental sustainability.

## Foundational Texts

### Al-Sarihi (2023)
**Title:** Political economy of renewable energy transition in rentier states: The case of Oman
**Author:** Aisha Al-Sarihi
**Source:** *Environmental Policy and Governance*, 33(3), 2023
**Notes:** Analyses the political-economic barriers preventing GCC petrostates from using
JV-style renewable energy partnerships to decarbonise, focusing on incumbent fossil-fuel
interests, subsidy lock-in, and state capitalism dynamics. Provides the comparative
MENA-petrostate context for understanding why UAE JV governance (ADNOC + ICV + Masdar JV
formation) is structured differently from Norway's CCS-focused JV strategy, and how each
addresses the trilemma's affordability–sustainability trade-off. The Oman analogy is
particularly useful because Oman's PDO structure (Shell 34%, Total 4%, Partex 2%, Oman
government 60%) is closer to ADNOC's concession-JV model than Norway's equal-share NCS
partnerships.

### IEA (2020)
**Title:** CCUS in Clean Energy Transitions
**Source:** International Energy Agency (IEA), Paris
**URL:** <https://www.iea.org/reports/ccus-in-clean-energy-transitions>
**Notes:** Foundational IEA report on the role of CCUS technology in achieving net-zero
scenarios. Documents that oil and gas JVs represent roughly 90% of global CCUS project
capacity in operation and under development, making JV governance the primary delivery
mechanism for the sustainability pillar's most capital-intensive decarbonisation pathway.
Directly relevant for understanding how CCS JVs (Northern Lights, Abu Dhabi CCS Hub)
constitute the key mechanism through which upstream JV governance contributes to the
trilemma's sustainability dimension. The report's conclusion — that CCUS is a "must-have"
technology under any credible net-zero pathway — validates the analytical centrality of
the CCUS module in this repository.

> and [`analysis/norway-ccus-jv-joa-governance.md`](../../analysis/norway-ccus-jv-joa-governance.md).

### Martin, Gilbert & Gusy (2020)
**Title:** A global review of joint operating agreement disputes
**Authors:** Timothy Martin; Jeremy Gilbert; Markus Gusy
**Source:** *Journal of World Energy Law & Business*, 13(3), 205–259, 2020
**Notes:** The most comprehensive empirical survey of JOA disputes in upstream oil and gas,
covering reported court judgments and international arbitral awards globally. Directly
relevant for identifying governance weaknesses in JOA design — voting thresholds, default
clauses, operator liability, cash-call mechanics — that affect whether JV structures can
meet sustainability and security objectives simultaneously. The study finds that disputes
arising from operator default and non-operator information-access failures are the two most
common categories; both failure modes are structurally more likely in UAE-style
NOC-majority concession JVs (where information asymmetry is high) than in Norway-style
licensed partnerships (where NPD data regulations reduce asymmetry). Benchmarks NOROG
model JOA provisions against international practice.

### Thurber (2012)
**Title:** Handing the NOC the reins: Governance implications of the shift from joint ventures to concessions in oil and gas
**Author:** Mark C. Thurber
**Source:** In: Victor, D.G., Hults, D.R. & Thurber, M.C. (eds), *Oil and Governance*.
Cambridge University Press, 2012
**Notes:** Examines how the structural shift in upstream deal architecture — from
equal-equity JVs toward NOC-majority concession/service contracts — affects energy security
policy instrumentalisation. Frames the ADNOC model (where JVs are always NOC-majority
concessions with IOC minority stakes) as a deliberate energy security tool that maximises
state resource control at the cost of governance transparency and IOC accountability.
Contrasts with Norway's commercially balanced JV regime, where the state maintains
influence through SDFI direct participation rather than through NOC equity majority.
Directly relevant to understanding how JOA design embeds — or constrains — trilemma pillar
trade-offs at the contractual level.

### Victor, Hults & Thurber (eds) (2012)
**Title:** Oil and Governance: State-Owned Enterprises and the World Energy Supply
**Source:** Cambridge University Press, 2012
**Notes:** Landmark comparative volume on NOC governance and its relationship to energy
supply security. The ADNOC case study documents how ADNOC used its concession JV
structures — requiring IOC equity participation as a technology-transfer mechanism — as
instruments of energy security, anticipating the ICV programme logic that now requires
45%+ in-country value in ADNOC contracts. Directly addresses whether JV governance
arrangements can simultaneously serve commercial returns, security of supply, and longer-
term sustainability ambitions, finding that NOC-majority structures systematically favour
security over sustainability. The volume's comparative framework is the starting point for
situating the Norway–UAE governance comparison within the global NOC literature.

### Wettestad, Inderberg & Gulbrandsen (2024)
**Title:** Exploring paths and innovation in Norwegian carbon capture and storage policy
**Authors:** Jørgen Wettestad; Tor Håkon Jackson Inderberg; Lars H. Gulbrandsen
**Source:** *Environmental Policy and Governance*, 34(1), 2024
**Notes:** Documents how Northern Lights — structured as a tripartite JV between Equinor,
Shell, and TotalEnergies under Norwegian state coordination — pioneered the first
commercially viable cross-border CCUS joint venture and represents the highest-profile case
of the trilemma's sustainability pillar being advanced through JV governance design rather
than regulation alone. The paper traces the policy path dependency from Sleipner (1996) to
Snøhvit (2008) to Longship/Northern Lights (2020–), showing how each iteration embedded
stronger JV-based governance structures. Directly embodies the trilemma logic of using JV
structures to balance continued gas production (security/affordability) with CO₂ abatement
(sustainability) — the core analytical tension of the thesis.

> **Cross-reference:** See [`literature/by-theme/jv-governance.md`](jv-governance.md#norway-ccs-jv--northern-lights)
> for the full Northern Lights source register.

### Khan et al. (2022)
**Title:** World energy trilemma and transformative energy developments as determinants of economic growth amid environmental sustainability
**Authors:** Itbar Khan; Ahmed Zakari; Vishal Dagar; Surendar Singh
**Source:** *Energy Economics*, 108, 105884, 2022
**Notes:** Quantitative panel study testing whether trilemma performance (using the WEC
index) is a significant determinant of economic growth across 34 countries, including
Norway and UAE. Finds that energy equity and environmental sustainability improvements
drive growth, while energy security alone is insufficient — a finding relevant to the
thesis argument that UAE JV governance structures, which optimise for security, may leave
sustainability-driven growth on the table. The study provides the econometric complement
to the qualitative governance comparison: Norway's higher trilemma score predicts better
long-run economic performance, which in turn justifies the governance investment in
institutions like the SDFI, NPD data regulations, and the Longship programme.

## Analytical Framework

The central research question this theme serves: **do JV governance structures in Norway
and the UAE advance or undermine each trilemma pillar, and how do the two models compare?**

| Trilemma Pillar | JV Contribution Mechanisms | Key Tensions |
|-----------------|---------------------------|--------------|
| **Security** | Risk-pooling accelerates field development; NOC-IOC JVs transfer technology and operational capacity | Concentrated operatorship creates single-point-of-failure; geopolitical exposure of IOC partners (e.g. Lukoil's Ghasha stake) |
| **Equity** | ICV obligations in JV contracts; local content and workforce development | Revenue-sharing structures may prioritise IOC returns over host-state equity goals; subsidy lock-in in petrostates |
| **Sustainability** | CCS JVs (Northern Lights); ESG clauses in AIEN 2023 JOA; GHG reporting obligations | Greenfield hydrocarbon JVs lock in fossil-fuel infrastructure; CCS JOAs not designed for multi-decade post-closure obligations |

## Key Themes to Track

- JV structures as instruments of energy security policy (Norway SDFI vs UAE concession equity)
- ICV programmes as equity mechanisms in UAE upstream JVs
- CCS/CCUS JVs as sustainability vehicles (Northern Lights as case study)
- Green JV formation trends (Ankura Index data) — renewables, hydrogen, offshore wind
- Trilemma trade-offs embedded in JOA design: how governance terms reflect pillar priorities
- State-NOC vs IOC incentive misalignment across trilemma dimensions
- Norway and UAE comparative performance on WEC Trilemma Index
