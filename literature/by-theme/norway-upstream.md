# Literature: Norway Upstream Sector

Annotated reading list on Norwegian upstream petroleum governance, regulation, and policy.

## Foundational Texts

**Nakhle, C. (2010)** Petroleum fiscal regimes: Evolution and challenges, in Daniel, P., Keen, M. & McPherson, C. (eds), *The Taxation of Petroleum and Minerals: Principles, Problems and Practice*. Routledge / IMF.

Analyses comparative petroleum fiscal regimes — licence-and-royalty, production-sharing contracts, service contracts — and their economic effects, with Norway's 78% marginal tax rate and SDFI model as a principal case. Essential for understanding how Norway's unique fiscal design shapes JV partner incentives and risk allocation, and how it differs from Abu Dhabi's concession-plus-NOC-equity model.

---

**Stoknes, P.E., Aslaksen, I., Goluke, U., Randers, J. & Garnåsjordet, P.A. (2024)** Plausible futures for the Norwegian offshore energy sector: Business as usual, harvest or rebuild?, *Energy Policy*, 184, 113887.

Models three investment scenarios for the NCS from 2020 to 2070: business-as-usual, harvest-and-exit, and green offshore rebuild. Provides quantitative framing for the trilemma trade-off facing Norwegian JV operators — whether continued upstream JVs maximise rent extraction or delay decarbonisation — and gives empirical grounding for the "harvest" option favoured by some NCS partners.

---

**Thurber, M.C., Hults, D.R. & Heller, P.R.P. (2011)** Exporting the 'Norwegian Model': The effect of administrative design on oil sector performance, *Energy Policy*, 39(9), 5366–5378.

Studies ten countries that attempted to replicate Norway's tripartite separation of NOC/ministry/regulator functions and finds separation alone does not produce good outcomes — institutional capacity and political competition are the decisive factors. Directly relevant for benchmarking Norway's NCS governance against the UAE's more centralised ADNOC–Supreme Petroleum Council model, and for explaining why the Norwegian JV licensing framework performs as it does.

---

**Tordo, S., Tracy, B.S. & Arfaa, N. (2011)** *National Oil Companies and Value Creation*. World Bank Working Paper No. 217. World Bank.

Analyses 20 NOCs across net-importing and net-exporting countries for governance design, objectives, and performance; Equinor/Statoil is a central case. Provides the governance-performance matrix for evaluating how state operator design in Norway enables JV structures that balance commercial returns (Equinor's equity interests) with state policy objectives (CO₂ tax, SDFI, energy security), directly applicable to Norway/UAE comparison.

---

**Victor, D.G., Hults, D.R. & Thurber, M.C. (eds) (2012)** *Oil and Governance: State-Owned Enterprises and the World Energy Supply*. Cambridge University Press.

Fifteen case studies — including Equinor/Statoil and ADNOC — of NOC governance structures, analysing how differing degrees of commercialisation, state control, and institutional design affect NOC performance. Establishes the comparative analytical framework within which Norwegian and Emirati JV governance can be assessed, particularly regarding the operator/state separation that underpins Norwegian JOAs.

---

**Wettestad, J., Inderberg, T.H.J. & Gulbrandsen, L.H. (2024)** Exploring paths and innovation in Norwegian carbon capture and storage policy, *Environmental Policy and Governance*, 34(1).

Traces how Norway pivoted from the failed Mongstad CCS mega-project (terminated 2013) to the successful Longship/Northern Lights JV structure (operational 2024). Illustrates how a multiparty JV (Equinor, Shell, TotalEnergies) under government coordination serves as an instrument for meeting Norway's trilemma sustainability obligations while preserving upstream commercial interests. Relevant to both Norway Upstream and JV Contribution themes.
