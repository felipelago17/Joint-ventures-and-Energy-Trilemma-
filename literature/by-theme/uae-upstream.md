# Literature: UAE Upstream Sector

Annotated reading list on UAE upstream petroleum governance, ADNOC concession structures, and federal/emirate-level regulation.

## Foundational Texts

### Palacios & Vidotto Caricati (2023)
**Title:** Assessing ESG Risks in National Oil Companies: Transcending ESG Ratings with a Better Understanding of Governance
**Authors:** Luisa Palacios; Catarina Vidotto Caricati
**Source:** Columbia University Center on Global Energy Policy (CGEP), May 18 2023
**URL:** <https://www.energypolicy.columbia.edu/publications/assessing-esg-risks-in-national-oil-companies-transcending-esg-ratings-with-a-better-understanding-of-governance/>
**PDF:** <https://www.energypolicy.columbia.edu/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/ESGRatingsforNOCs-CGEP_Report_050923-1.pdf>
**Notes:** Provides the most rigorous publicly available ESG risk framework specifically calibrated to NOCs, covering companies that collectively produce ~50% of global oil output, hold >50% of global refining capacity, and own the bulk of proven reserves. **ADNOC-specific findings:** ADNOC is identified as an outlier case — it does not publish financial statements or standardised ESG reports, making it unrateable by conventional ESG scoring agencies. While ADNOC communicates high-level decarbonisation targets publicly (net-zero by 2045, methane intensity reductions), these claims are unsupported by disclosed underlying metrics, creating a verification gap that is structurally distinct from IOC or listed-NOC peers. This gap is directly relevant to JV governance: IOC partners operating within ADNOC concession structures cannot independently audit environmental performance commitments embedded in joint-venture agreements. **Governance-environment linkage:** The report's central argument is that conventional ESG ratings fail NOCs because they treat environmental performance as separable from governance structure; in practice, disclosure opacity is itself a governance failure that masks environmental risk. Palacios and Vidotto Caricati propose a "governance-adjusted ESG" approach that treats state ownership concentration, board independence, and transparency regime as first-order risk drivers — not merely background factors. **State-ownership governance framework:** The study finds that stock-market listing is the single most powerful lever for improving NOC governance and ESG performance, as it imposes external disclosure obligations and activates minority-shareholder accountability mechanisms. Independent domestic regulators (separate from the NOC's political principal) and professionally constituted boards of directors with independent directors are identified as secondary but important levers. ADNOC's partial IPO of subsidiaries (ADNOC Distribution, ADNOC Drilling, ADNOC Gas) is noted as a partial step, but the parent company's non-listed status preserves the core disclosure gap. **Comparison relevance:** Equinor is implicitly the counter-model — a listed NOC subject to Oslo Bourse disclosure rules, PSA regulatory oversight, and Norwegian state stewardship expectations — illustrating the governance distance between the Norwegian and UAE upstream models. A key implication for JV governance research is that the choice of NOC partner (listed vs non-listed) shapes the information environment available to IOC co-venturers and regulators alike.
