# Literature: Joint-Venture Governance

Annotated reading list for joint-venture governance in the upstream petroleum sector.

## Foundational Texts

### Ankura (2023a)
**Title:** JV and Partnership Volume Continues Upward Trajectory — Ankura Joint Venture Index H1 2023
**Source:** Ankura Joint Venture Alchemist (jvalchemist.ankura.com)
**URL:** <https://jvalchemist.ankura.com/ankura-joint-venture-index/ankura-joint-venture-index-h1-2023/>
**Notes:** Tracks ~1,500 global JV and partnership transactions per half-year. H1 2023 volumes ran 40% ahead of H1 2022 even as global M&A activity fell to a record low, indicating that collaborative deal structures are increasingly preferred over outright acquisitions. Climate-focused deals accounted for 61% of new JV/partnership volumes in H1 2023, up from only 19% three years prior — green energy sectors (hydrogen/ammonia, CCS, offshore wind, bio-fuels, EVs) drove the majority of new formation activity. Contingent pricing terms — rare outside pharma/biotech — appeared with greater frequency as parties bridged divergent valuation assumptions. The ADNOC/Masdar restructuring is documented as a high-profile UAE case: Abu Dhabi consolidated its clean energy assets into a tripartite JV (ADNOC 43%, Mubadala 33%, TAQA 24%), illustrating how a state NOC can use JV restructuring to impose portfolio coherence and access scaled capital for green transition without relinquishing majority control.

### Ankura (2023b)
**Title:** 2023 In Review — And What Lies Ahead
**Source:** Ankura Joint Venture Alchemist (jvalchemist.ankura.com)
**URL:** <https://jvalchemist.ankura.com/ankura-joint-venture-index/2023-in-review-and-what-lies-ahead/>
**Notes:** Full-year 2023 retrospective confirming record JV/partnership formation volumes. Green JVs sustained the 61% share of new deal flow established in H1, with hydrogen/ammonia and CCS as the highest-growth sub-sectors. The report specifically flags a pattern of natural-resource JV restructurings triggered by host-country governments — a structural governance dynamic directly relevant to the Norway/UAE comparison, where the NPD licensing framework and ADNOC's concession model represent two distinct state-driven restructuring logics. "Bold, billion-dollar bets on new businesses" characterised many 2023 deals, including offshore wind and sustainable aviation fuel partnerships. The year-end report also notes the rise of AI-enabled JV operations and digital-capability partnerships, foreshadowing the computational sovereignty dimension of state–IOC data-sharing arrangements.

### AIEN (2023)
**Title:** AIEN 2023 International Model Joint Operating Agreement
**Source:** Association of International Energy Negotiators (AIEN, formerly AIPN)
**URL:** <https://www.aien.org/forms/store/ProductFormPublic/joint-operating-agreement-2023>
**TOC (open access):** <https://www.aien.org/forms/uploadFiles/A844E00000031.toc.TOC_AIEN_Model_Joint_Operating_Agreement_2023.pdf>
**Commentary (V&E):** <https://www.velaw.com/insights/the-2023-aien-model-international-joint-operating-agreement/>
**Commentary (Bracewell):** <https://www.bracewell.com/resources/2023-aien-model-form-joa-placing-esg-issues-heart-oil-gas-operations/>
**Notes:** Supersedes the widely-used 2012 AIPN Model JOA; designed to work alongside the 2020 Unitization and Unit Operating Agreement. The 2023 revision is significant for this research on three counts. **Governance architecture:** The model retains the operator/operating-committee structure with differentiated voting thresholds for routine, significant, and exceptional decisions; Article 12 treats transfers and changes of control as distinct subjects, relevant where NOC parent restructuring triggers a deemed assignment. **ESG provisions (new in 2023):** Optional GHG emissions clauses impose an operator duty to limit/mitigate emissions and to report periodically — provisions more naturally adopted in Norway (where a carbon tax of ~US$70/tCO₂ creates direct incentive) than in UAE (where no equivalent internalisation mechanism exists). New human rights obligations are anchored to the UN Declaration of Human Rights, ILO Declaration of Fundamental Principles, and UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights; the operator must manage operations in conformity and all parties must exercise JOA rights consistently with human rights standards. **Sanctions:** A new dedicated article makes sanctions compliance an operator obligation, permits removal of a sanctioned operator, prohibits transfers to sanctioned entities (treated as deemed default), and grants any party a withdrawal right if it reasonably believes another party is subject to sanctions — directly relevant to IOC partners operating in the UAE context. **Decommissioning:** Non-operators can now compel the operator to submit decommissioning cost estimates; an independent expert can be invoked; and Exhibit E introduces "Legacy Wells" provisions for wells pre-dating the JOA. **Norway/UAE comparison relevance:** The 2023 AIEN model offers a useful benchmark: Norwegian JOAs broadly align with or exceed its ESG and transparency standards (reinforced by NPD data regulations and the PSA framework), whereas UAE/ADNOC concession JV structures have historically operated below the AIEN transparency baseline, particularly on GHG reporting and third-party data access.

## Norway CCS JV — Northern Lights

The Northern Lights JV (Equinor 33.3%, Shell 33.3%, TotalEnergies 33.3%) is the world's first commercial open-source CO₂ transport and storage infrastructure, operating under the Norwegian Government's *Longship* CCS programme. It is the primary empirical case for CCUS JV governance on the Norwegian Continental Shelf and illustrates how state funding, NOC-led operatorship, and a dedicated regulatory framework interact in a tripartite IOC joint venture.

### Northern Lights JV (ongoing)
**Title:** Northern Lights — Reports, Analysis and Assessments (official publications portal)
**Source:** norlights.com
**URL:** <https://norlights.com/reports-2/>
**Notes:** Canonical starting point for all primary Northern Lights JV publications: annual reports, carbon footprint assessments, monitoring studies, and technical briefings. The 2024 Annual Report confirms Phase 1 operational readiness (1.5 Mtpa CO₂ capacity; first CO₂ injection from Heidelberg Materials in summer 2025) and the March 2025 FID for Phase 2 expansion to ≥5 Mtpa at a total investment of NOK 7.5 billion. Key governance lesson highlighted across reports: successful CCS JV development requires early alignment across the full value chain, supported by clear milestones, strong policy backing, and mutual technical understanding between operator and non-operating partners.

### Northern Lights Project Concept Report (2020)
**Title:** Northern Lights Project Concept Report RE-PM673-00001
**Source:** CCS Norway (Longship project) / Gassnova
**URL:** <https://ccsnorway.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/6/2020/05/Northern-Lights-Project-Concept-report.pdf>
**Notes:** Foundational pre-FID governance document. Describes the JV formation mechanism: pre-FID work governed by a study agreement between Gassnova (Norwegian state representative) and Equinor; a separate collaboration agreement between Equinor, Shell, and Total (now TotalEnergies) governed study work and provided the template for the Joint Venture Agreement upon a positive investment decision. Establishes the "open source" transport and storage infrastructure model, allowing third-party CO₂ from multiple industrial emitters — a governance design with no direct analogue in UAE upstream JV practice. The state funding arrangement (Norwegian Government covering 80% of Phase 1 investment costs) and Gassnova's coordinationg role as the state's contractual counterpart are central to understanding how this JV differs from a standard commercial upstream JOA.

### Northern Lights Carbon Footprint Report (2023)
**Title:** Carbon Footprint of the Northern Lights JV CO₂ Transport and Storage Value Chain
**Source:** Northern Lights JV
**URL:** <https://norlights.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/Report-Carbon-footprint-of-the-Northern-Lights-JV-co2-transport-and-storage-value-chain.pdf>
**Notes:** Full lifecycle GHG assessment from construction through decommissioning for Phase 1 and Phase 2 combined (minimum 5 Mtpa over 25 years). Key quantitative findings: 127.8 Mt CO₂ stored over asset lifetime; lifecycle emissions of 3.3 Mt (2.6% of stored volume); net abatement of 124.5 Mt CO₂ (97.4% net efficiency). The dominant emission source is ship fuel consumption during CO₂ transport; the JV has adopted LNG propulsion, wind-assisted rotor sails, and air lubrication to reduce this footprint. Directly relevant to the AIEN 2023 JOA's optional GHG reporting provisions: Northern Lights operates at a transparency standard that significantly exceeds current ADNOC JV practice, demonstrating what full lifecycle GHG disclosure in a CCS JV context can look like.

### Gassnova Annual Benefits Realisation Report (2025)
**Title:** Annual Benefits Realisation Report — Northern Lights to Gassnova
**Source:** Gassnova (Norwegian state enterprise for CCS)
**URL:** <https://gassnova.no/app/uploads/sites/6/2025/04/Annual-Benefits-Realisation-Report-Northern-Lights-to-Gassnova.pdf>
**Notes:** Illustrates the state-oversight governance layer sitting above the JV. Gassnova, acting on behalf of the Norwegian Ministry of Energy, runs a structured "benefits realisation" programme requiring the Northern Lights JV to report annually on whether the state's investment objectives are being met. This is a contractual accountability mechanism appended to the state aid agreement — not a feature of a standard commercial JOA — and represents a model for how a host state can maintain real-time performance oversight over a JV without holding equity. The 2025 report covers the commencement of CO₂ transport and injection operations, marking realisation of the full Longship value chain. Relevant to the thesis argument that Norwegian upstream JV governance embeds state accountability tools that have no equivalent in UAE ADNOC concession structures.

### Gassnova Annual Experience Transfer Report 2023 (2024)
**Title:** Annual Experience Transfer Report 2023 — Northern Lights Phase 1 Facilities
**Source:** Gassnova / Northern Lights JV
**URL:** <https://gassnova.no/app/uploads/sites/6/2024/04/PM673-JV-A-PG-00015_02-Annual-experience-transfer-report-2023-for-Northern-Lights-Phase-1-Facilities.pdf>
**Notes:** Contractually mandated knowledge-sharing report, covering January–December 2023. Reflects a JV governance provision requiring the operator to transfer operational experience to Gassnova (and thereby to the Norwegian state and wider CCS community) on an annual basis. Key operational lessons documented: (1) quay/ship interface design misalignment requiring costly late-stage modifications — lesson for JOA scope-definition and change-order governance; (2) pipeline cleanliness below specification after commissioning pigging — lesson for operator standard-of-care obligations; (3) flange corrosion from inadequate outdoor storage protocols; (4) need for CCS-specific technical specifications rather than hydrocarbon-derived standards, which caused overdesign and cost overruns. The reporting obligation itself is significant: it externalises JV operational intelligence to the state, reducing information asymmetry in a way that Norwegian regulatory design mandates but ADNOC concession structures do not require.

### Røsjorde & Carpenter / NETL — The Norwegian Full-Scale CCS Project (2020)
**Title:** The Norwegian Full-Scale CCS Project
**Source:** Audun Røsjorde; Mike Carpenter; NETL 2020 Integrated Project Review Meeting
**URL:** <https://netl.doe.gov/sites/default/files/netl-file/20CCUS_Carpenter.pdf>
**Notes:** Presented to the US Department of Energy's NETL, this document summarises the Norwegian Longship/Northern Lights governance model for an international audience. Critical governance details: the Norwegian Government funds 80% of Phase 1 investment costs and up to 95% of operational costs, with each JV partner bearing risk only within its own contractual scope — a deliberate risk-partitioning mechanism designed to overcome the chicken-and-egg problem of first-mover CCS investment. Long-term liability for stored CO₂ transfers to the Norwegian Government upon site closure under Norwegian law (Petroleum Act and CO₂ Storage Regulations), de-risking the JV's long-tail exposure. This state-as-residual-risk-bearer model is a distinctive feature of Norwegian CCS JV governance that fundamentally shapes partner incentives, and contrasts with UAE upstream JV structures where long-term decommissioning and storage liability allocation remains less developed.

**Title:** Northern Lights [CCUS Hub Case Study]
**Source:** Oil and Gas Climate Initiative (OGCI) CCUS Hub
**URL:** <https://ccushub.ogci.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/Northern-Lights.pdf>
**Notes:** OGCI frames Northern Lights as the reference case for the "contractor to the state" CCUS hub model — Phase 1 operates under a state-aid structure where government bears 80% of investment and 95% of operational costs, with the JV responsible for commercialising the capacity on an open-access basis. ## UAE CCS JV — Al Reyadah

Al Reyadah is the world's first CCUS project capturing CO₂ from the iron and steel industry and the first commercial-scale CCUS project in the MENA region, commissioned in late 2016. Originally structured as a JV between ADNOC (51%) and Masdar (49%), it captures CO₂ from the Emirates Steel Industries (ESI) DRI plant at Mussafah, transports it via a 45 km pipeline, and injects it into ADNOC's onshore oil fields for enhanced oil recovery (EOR). ADNOC subsequently acquired Masdar's 49% stake, converting Al Reyadah into a wholly-owned ADNOC subsidiary integrated into ADNOC Onshore — a JV governance lifecycle event (formation → operation → NOC absorption) with no equivalent in the Norwegian CCS model.

> **Cross-reference:** For the Norwegian comparative case — Northern Lights JV (Equinor / Shell / TotalEnergies, 33.3% each, commissioned 2025) — see the **Norway CCS JV — Northern Lights** section above.

**URL:** <https://ieefa.org/sites/default/files/2024-11/BN_Steel%20CCUS%20update-%20Carbon%20capture%20technology%20looks%20ever%20less%20convincing_Nov24.pdf>
**Notes:** The most critical publicly available independent assessment of Al Reyadah's operational performance, published eight years after commissioning. Key findings: (1) Al Reyadah remains the world's only operational commercial-scale CCUS facility for steelmaking, with zero replication over eight years despite its "first-mover" status — evidence that its governance and commercial model has not been replicable; (2) in 2023 it captured only 26.6% of Emirates Steel Arkan's total Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions, far below the 800,000 t/yr nameplate capacity and the broader decarbonisation claims made at commissioning; (3) in 2020–2021 the facility captured less than 20% of the plant's emissions; (4) the CO₂ is used for EOR, enabling additional fossil fuel production — IEEFA characterises this as a net climate disbenefit. The report also notes that Emirates Steel Arkan is now developing a DRI-EAF green hydrogen pilot, effectively moving away from CCS. This is essential counter-evidence to ADNOC's sustainability report narrative and directly relevant to the JV governance-environment linkage argument: without mandatory independent reporting obligations (as exist in Norway's CCS regulatory framework), governance opacity allows persistent underperformance to go unchallenged.
