    Literature: UAE Upstream Sector¶

    Annotated reading list on UAE upstream petroleum governance, ADNOC concession structures, and federal/emirate-level regulation.

    Foundational Texts¶

    Palacios & Vidotto Caricati (2023)¶

    Title: Assessing ESG Risks in National Oil Companies: Transcending ESG Ratings with a Better Understanding of Governance Authors: Luisa Palacios; Catarina Vidotto Caricati Source: Columbia University Center on Global Energy Policy (CGEP), May 18 2023 URL: https://www.energypolicy.columbia.edu/publications/assessing-esg-risks-in-national-oil-companies-transcending-esg-ratings-with-a-better-understanding-of-governance/ PDF: https://www.energypolicy.columbia.edu/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/ESGRatingsforNOCs-CGEP_Report_050923-1.pdf Notes: Provides the most rigorous publicly available ESG risk framework specifically calibrated to NOCs, covering companies that collectively produce ~50% of global oil output, hold >50% of global refining capacity, and own the bulk of proven reserves. ADNOC-specific findings: ADNOC is identified as an outlier case — it does not publish financial statements or standardised ESG reports, making it unrateable by conventional ESG scoring agencies. While ADNOC communicates high-level decarbonisation targets publicly (net-zero by 2045, methane intensity reductions), these claims are unsupported by disclosed underlying metrics, creating a verification gap that is structurally distinct from IOC or listed-NOC peers. This gap is directly relevant to JV governance: IOC partners operating within ADNOC concession structures cannot independently audit environmental performance commitments embedded in joint-venture agreements. Governance-environment linkage: The report's central argument is that conventional ESG ratings fail NOCs because they treat environmental performance as separable from governance structure; in practice, disclosure opacity is itself a governance failure that masks environmental risk. Palacios and Vidotto Caricati propose a "governance-adjusted ESG" approach that treats state ownership concentration, board independence, and transparency regime as first-order risk drivers — not merely background factors. State-ownership governance framework: The study finds that stock-market listing is the single most powerful lever for improving NOC governance and ESG performance, as it imposes external disclosure obligations and activates minority-shareholder accountability mechanisms. Independent domestic regulators (separate from the NOC's political principal) and professionally constituted boards of directors with independent directors are identified as secondary but important levers. ADNOC's partial IPO of subsidiaries (ADNOC Distribution, ADNOC Drilling, ADNOC Gas) is noted as a partial step, but the parent company's non-listed status preserves the core disclosure gap. Comparison relevance: Equinor is implicitly the counter-model — a listed NOC subject to Oslo Bourse disclosure rules, PSA regulatory oversight, and Norwegian state stewardship expectations — illustrating the governance distance between the Norwegian and UAE upstream models. A key implication for JV governance research is that the choice of NOC partner (listed vs non-listed) shapes the information environment available to IOC co-venturers and regulators alike.

    UAE Constitution — Article 23 (Resource Sovereignty)¶

    Title: Constitution of the United Arab Emirates, Article 23 Source: UAE Ministry of Justice / Federal National Council URL: https://u.ae/en/information-and-services/justice-safety-and-the-law/the-constitution-of-the-uae Notes: Article 23 declares that "Natural resources and wealth in each Emirate shall be considered the public property of that Emirate." This provision is the constitutional foundation for the emirate-level concession architecture that governs all upstream activities in Abu Dhabi (and by analogy Dubai and Sharjah). The legal consequence for JV governance is significant: there is no federal petroleum licensing framework analogous to Norway's NCS licence system administered by a national directorate. Instead, each emirate holds sovereign title and grants concessions through its own instrumentalities (for Abu Dhabi: the Supreme Petroleum Council and ADNOC). This constitutional structure means that upstream JV partners contract with Abu Dhabi as a sovereign, not with a federal licensing authority, and that pore space and storage rights — relevant for CCUS governance — derive from the same emirate-level title without a separate licensing layer.

    Cross-reference: This constitutional point underpins Section 1 of the UAE CCUS governance analysis at analysis/uae-ccus-jv-joa-governance.md.

    Chambers and Partners (2025)¶

    Title: Oil, Gas and the Transition to Renewables 2025 — United Arab Emirates Source: Chambers and Partners, Global Practice Guides URL: https://practiceguides.chambers.com/practice-guides/oil-gas-and-the-transition-to-renewables-2025/united-arab-emirates Notes: Annually updated practitioner-authored country chapter providing the most accessible comprehensive summary of UAE petroleum law for non-specialist readers. Covers: the constitutional framework (Article 23); the Abu Dhabi concession model (ADNOC as national company and co-concessionaire); the role of the Supreme Petroleum Council; participation interest structures (ADNOC typically holds 60%+ in upstream concessions); data and IP ownership in JOAs (ADNOC retains data rights); and recent regulatory developments including the UAE Climate Law mandatory reporting provisions effective from 2026. The absence of a separate petroleum regulator (the Supreme Petroleum Council is both policy-setter and ultimate owner through ADNOC) is highlighted as a structural distinction from jurisdictions with independent regulators — a governance gap that affects the information environment for IOC co-venturers. Useful as an annual-updating reference to track regulatory evolution.

    Norton Rose Fulbright (n.d.)¶

    Title: Understanding hydrogen and CCS in the UAE Source: Norton Rose Fulbright (international law firm) URL: https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en/knowledge/publications/159c6aad/understanding-hydrogen-and-ccs-in-the-uae Notes: Practitioner briefing paper covering the legal architecture for hydrogen projects and CCUS in the UAE. The paper confirms that there is no standalone CCS storage statute — storage rights derive from the petroleum concession framework and emirate-level sovereign title. Key governance points: (a) CCUS projects are currently structured as part of upstream petroleum concessions rather than as standalone storage ventures; (b) the UAE Climate Law (Federal Law No. 11 of 2023) establishes a national GHG reporting obligation but does not create a dedicated CCUS regulatory framework; (c) the absence of a codified long-term liability-transfer mechanism for post-closure CO₂ storage is identified as the principal legal gap to be addressed if the UAE is to scale up CCUS in a bankable manner. This practitioner analysis supports the governance-gap argument in the CCUS module and is a citable secondary authority on UAE CCS law.

    ADNOC (2023)¶

    Title: ADNOC 2030 Smart Growth Strategy Source: Abu Dhabi National Oil Company (ADNOC) URL: https://www.adnoc.ae/en/our-company/our-strategy Notes: ADNOC's publicly stated strategic framework targets production capacity of 5 million barrels per day by 2027 and 5.5 million bpd by 2030, alongside decarbonisation objectives (net zero by 2045, doubling CCS capacity to 10 mtpa by 2030, reducing methane intensity). The strategy is implemented through a portfolio of IOC joint ventures — ADNOC Offshore (with Total, BP, Shell, INPEX), ADNOC Onshore (with BP, Shell, Total, Partex), ADNOC Gas, and project-specific vehicles including the Hail and Ghasha consortium. For the thesis, the 2030 Strategy is notable because it frames IOC partnership as primarily a technology and capital mechanism rather than a governance mechanism — ADNOC controls operatorship and data, and IOC partners provide capital, technology licences, and reputational capacity for international debt markets. This structuring logic contrasts with Norway's NCS model, where IOC participation was historically engineered to build domestic technical capability within a shared governance framework.
