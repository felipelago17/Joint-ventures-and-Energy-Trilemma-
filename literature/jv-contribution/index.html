    Literature: Contribution of Joint Ventures to the Energy Trilemma¶

    Annotated reading list on how joint-venture structures in the upstream petroleum sector contribute to — or create tensions within — the three pillars of the WEC energy trilemma: energy security, energy equity, and environmental sustainability.

    Foundational Texts¶

    Al-Sarihi, A. (2023) Political economy of renewable energy transition in rentier states: The case of Oman, Environmental Policy and Governance, 33(3).

    Analyses the political-economic barriers preventing GCC petrostates from using JV-style renewable energy partnerships to decarbonise, focusing on incumbent fossil-fuel interests, subsidy lock-in, and state capitalism dynamics. Provides the comparative MENA-petrostate context for understanding why UAE JV governance (ADNOC + ICV + Masdar JV formation) is structured differently from Norway's CCS-focused JV strategy, and how each addresses the trilemma's affordability–sustainability trade-off.

    IEA (2020) CCUS in Clean Energy Transitions. International Energy Agency. Available at: https://www.iea.org/reports/ccus-in-clean-energy-transitions

    Foundational IEA report on the role of CCUS technology in achieving net-zero; documents that oil and gas JVs represent roughly 90% of global CCUS project capacity in operation and under development. Directly relevant for understanding how CCS JVs (e.g., Northern Lights, Abu Dhabi CCS Hub) constitute the key mechanism through which upstream JV governance contributes to the trilemma's sustainability dimension.

    Martin, T., Gilbert, J. & Gusy, M. (2020) A global review of joint operating agreement disputes, Journal of World Energy Law & Business, 13(3), 205–259.

    The most comprehensive empirical survey of JOA disputes in upstream oil and gas, covering reported court judgments and international arbitral awards globally. Directly relevant for identifying governance weaknesses in JOA design — voting thresholds, default clauses, operator liability — that affect whether JV structures can meet sustainability and security objectives simultaneously, and for benchmarking NOROG model JOA provisions against international practice.

    Thurber, M.C. (2012) Handing the NOC the reins: Governance implications of the shift from joint ventures to concessions in oil and gas, in Victor, Hults & Thurber (eds), Oil and Governance. Cambridge University Press.

    Examines how the structural shift in upstream deal architecture — from equal-equity JVs toward NOC-majority concession/service contracts — affects energy security policy instrumentalisation. Frames the ADNOC model (where JVs are always NOC-majority concessions with IOC minority stakes) as a deliberate energy security tool, contrasting with Norway's more commercially balanced JV regime; relevant for how JOA design embeds trilemma priorities.

    Victor, D.G., Hults, D.R. & Thurber, M.C. (eds) (2012) Oil and Governance: State-Owned Enterprises and the World Energy Supply. Cambridge University Press.

    The ADNOC case study in this volume documents how ADNOC used its concession JV structures — requiring IOC equity participation — as instruments of technology transfer and energy security, anticipating the ICV programme logic. Directly addresses whether JV governance arrangements can simultaneously serve commercial returns, security of supply, and longer-term sustainability ambitions.

    Wettestad, J., Inderberg, T.H.J. & Gulbrandsen, L.H. (2024) Exploring paths and innovation in Norwegian carbon capture and storage policy, Environmental Policy and Governance, 34(1).

    Documents how Northern Lights — structured as a tripartite JV between Equinor, Shell, and TotalEnergies under Norwegian state coordination — pioneered the first commercially viable cross-border CCUS joint venture. Directly embodies the trilemma logic of using JV structures to balance continued gas production (security/affordability) with CO₂ abatement (sustainability). See also Norway Upstream theme.

    Analytical Framework¶

    The central research question this theme serves: do JV governance structures in Norway and the UAE advance or undermine each trilemma pillar, and how do the two models compare?

    Trilemma Pillar JV Contribution Mechanisms Key Tensions
    Security Risk-pooling accelerates field development; NOC-IOC JVs transfer technology and operational capacity Concentrated operatorship creates single-point-of-failure; geopolitical exposure of IOC partners
    Equity In-country value (ICV) obligations in JV contracts; local content and workforce development Revenue-sharing structures may prioritise IOC returns over host-state equity goals
    Sustainability CCS JVs (e.g. Northern Lights); ESG clauses in JOAs; GHG reporting obligations Greenfield hydrocarbon JVs may lock in fossil-fuel infrastructure contrary to net-zero pathways

    Key Themes to Track¶

    • JV structures as instruments of energy security policy (Norway SDFI model vs UAE concession equity)
    • In-country value (ICV) programmes as equity mechanisms in UAE upstream JVs
    • CCS/CCUS JVs as sustainability vehicles (Northern Lights as case study)
    • Green JV formation trends (Ankura Index data) — renewables, hydrogen, offshore wind
    • Trilemma trade-offs embedded in JOA design: how governance terms reflect pillar priorities
    • State-NOC vs IOC incentive misalignment across trilemma dimensions
    • Norway and UAE comparative performance on WEC Trilemma Index
