    Literature: Energy Trilemma¶

    Annotated reading list on the World Energy Council energy trilemma framework — security, equity (affordability/access), and environmental sustainability.

    Foundational Framework¶

    World Energy Council (2023)¶

    Title: World Energy Trilemma Index 2023 Source: World Energy Council (WEC), London URL: https://trilemma.worldenergy.org/ Notes: The authoritative annual quantitative ranking of 130+ countries against the three trilemma dimensions: Energy Security (robustness of supply systems), Energy Equity (access and affordability), and Environmental Sustainability (decarbonisation and efficiency). The index weights each dimension equally and produces a composite trilemma score used to benchmark national energy systems. Norway consistently ranks in the top five globally — its high score reflects an abundant, diversified energy mix (hydropower + gas exports + emerging offshore wind), a strong regulatory apparatus, and high state capacity. UAE scores substantially lower on Environmental Sustainability (high per-capita emissions; hydrocarbon-dependent power sector) despite high Equity scores (heavily subsidised domestic energy prices) and moderate Security scores. The gap between Norway and UAE trilemma profiles is a central empirical anchor for the thesis: JV governance structures are one mechanism through which states either widen or narrow this gap.

    World Energy Council (2024)¶

    Title: World Energy Trilemma Index 2024 Source: World Energy Council (WEC), London URL: https://trilemma.worldenergy.org/ Notes: Most recent annual iteration. Captures the accelerating divergence between OECD and Gulf state trilemma trajectories post-Paris Agreement and following COP28 (hosted in Abu Dhabi, November–December 2023). The UAE's hosting of COP28 makes the 2024 index a particularly significant data point for the thesis: Abu Dhabi used the conference to launch updated NDC commitments and the Net Zero by 2045 objective, while simultaneously sanctioning the Habshan CCUS project — a combination that the WEC index will partially capture on the Sustainability dimension in subsequent years. Use this index to track whether ADNOC's CCUS investment pipeline translates into measurable trilemma-score improvement.

    World Energy Council (2023)¶

    Title: World Energy Issues Monitor 2023: Navigating the Energy Trilemma Source: World Energy Council (WEC), London URL: https://www.worldenergy.org/publications/entry/world-energy-issues-monitor-2023 Notes: Annual executive survey of ~2,000 energy leaders across 90 countries mapping the "critical uncertainties" and "decisive action" issues on each trilemma dimension. The 2023 Monitor is noteworthy for the thesis because: (a) geopolitical risk to energy security was ranked the number-one issue globally for the second consecutive year following Russia's invasion of Ukraine — highly relevant to Norwegian gas export policy and ADNOC's positioning as a reliable alternative supplier; (b) hydrogen/ammonia governance ranked as a decisive-action priority, intersecting with both the Hail & Ghasha gas-to-hydrogen pathway and Norway's NCS hydrogen ambitions; (c) AI and digitalisation were flagged as rising critical uncertainties — the entry point for the computational-sovereignty dimension of the thesis.

    Academic Definitions and Measurement¶

    Cherp & Jewell (2014)¶

    Title: The concept of energy security: Beyond the four As Authors: Aleh Cherp; Jessica Jewell Source: Energy Policy, Volume 75, December 2014 URL: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.enpol.2014.09.005 Notes: One of the most-cited definitional papers in energy security studies. Challenges the dominant "four As" framing (Availability, Accessibility, Affordability, Acceptability) as overlapping and under-theorised. Proposes instead a tripartite framework: (1) Sovereignty — control over energy systems and supply chains; (2) Robustness — ability of energy systems to withstand disruptions; (3) Resilience — capacity to adapt to energy-system change. This framing maps directly onto the thesis: sovereignty concerns explain Norway's insistence on the SDFI/Petoro model and the UAE's constitutional embedding of resource ownership in the emirate; robustness concerns explain JV governance structures around operatorship, default, and force majeure; resilience concerns explain how JV frameworks are (or are not) adapting to accommodate CCS and renewable integration. Cherp & Jewell's framework is more analytically tractable than the WEC trilemma for formal modelling purposes; the thesis benefits from using both in parallel.

    Sovacool & Mukherjee (2011)¶

    Title: Conceptualizing and measuring energy security: A synthesized approach Authors: Benjamin K. Sovacool; Ishaan Mukherjee Source: Energy, Volume 36, Issue 8, 2011 URL: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.energy.2011.01.011 Notes: Systematic review synthesising five competing conceptions of energy security from the literature and proposing a unified 20-indicator index. Particularly relevant to the thesis for two reasons. First, the paper identifies "governance quality" as an under-measured but crucial determinant of energy security outcomes — anticipating the governance-environment linkage central to the thesis. Second, Sovacool & Mukherjee find that OECD states consistently outperform hydrocarbon-rich developing states on their composite index, despite the latter's superior resource endowments, which is precisely the Norway–UAE divergence pattern the thesis investigates. A useful methodological reference if the thesis moves towards quantitative case comparison.

    Ang, Choong & Ng (2015)¶

    Title: Energy security: Definitions, dimensions and indexes Authors: B.W. Ang; W.L. Choong; T.S. Ng Source: Renewable and Sustainable Energy Reviews, Volume 42, February 2015 URL: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.rser.2014.10.064 Notes: Comprehensive meta-review of 104 energy security indexes published between 1990 and 2014. Finds that the field has accumulated a proliferating and inconsistent set of metrics, with no dominant standard. The paper identifies which dimensions are most frequently captured (supply diversity, import dependence, price volatility) and which are systematically neglected (governance capacity, environmental integration, long-run transition risk). The governance gap identified by Ang et al. is directly relevant: JV governance is not captured by any of the 104 indexes reviewed, suggesting that the thesis is filling a genuine measurement gap in the energy-security literature, not merely applying existing metrics to a new context.

    Energy Trilemma and the Energy Transition¶

    IEA (2024)¶

    Title: World Energy Outlook 2024 Source: International Energy Agency (IEA), Paris URL: https://www.iea.org/reports/world-energy-outlook-2024 Notes: The IEA's flagship annual scenario publication. The 2024 WEO is the most important single reference for situating the Norway–UAE comparison within global energy transition trajectories. Key findings relevant to the thesis: (a) the "Announced Pledges Scenario" (APS) pathway requires rapid acceleration of upstream decarbonisation, including CCUS deployment, across all producer states; (b) NOCs hold the majority of the world's remaining low-cost reserves and are therefore structurally pivotal to whether the 1.5°C pathway is achievable — a finding that elevates the governance of ADNOC JVs from a regional case study to a global transition question; (c) the IEA explicitly treats CCUS as a "must-have" technology for the APS, validating the analytical centrality of the UAE CCUS module to the trilemma sustainability dimension.

    Cross-reference: See also the UAE CCUS governance analysis at analysis/uae-ccus-jv-joa-governance.md for how CCUS JV/JOA structures interact with the sustainability dimension of the trilemma.

    Elkind (2010)¶

    Title: Energy Security: Call for a Broader Agenda Author: Jonathan Elkind Source: In: Pascual, C. & Elkind, J. (eds.), Energy Security: Economics, Politics, Strategies, and Implications. Brookings Institution Press, Washington DC, 2010. Notes: Chapter-length treatment arguing that energy security frameworks must incorporate environmental sustainability and energy access alongside the traditional supply-security focus — an early articulation of the trilemma logic. Elkind's "broader agenda" maps to the three WEC dimensions and provides historical context for why the trilemma framing emerged from the policy community rather than academia. Useful for the literature review section of the thesis to situate the WEC index within the intellectual history of energy security thought.

    Key Themes¶

    • Operationalisation of the trilemma in comparative policy analysis
    • Trade-offs between affordability and decarbonisation in petrostate contexts
    • Energy security in hydrocarbon-dependent economies undergoing transition
    • JV governance as a mechanism mediating trilemma trade-offs
    • Computational sovereignty as a fourth dimension (data/AI infrastructure)
